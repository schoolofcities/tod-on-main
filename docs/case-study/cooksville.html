How do you build a high-density community bound by natural and physical land restrictions?

Cooksville Station, Mississauga. A future transit super-hub where the GO train will meet the new Hazel McCallion LRT and Dundas Bus Rapid Transit (BRT) will converge, making it a focal point for massive growth.

Matching it is a lofty density target of 300 people and jobs per hectare.

However, limitations abound. At its periphery are long-standing neighbourhoods. It is also one of the most physically constrained parts of the city. Termed a “Flood Risk MTSA”, it has over 300 buildings sitting within the Cooksville Creek floodplain.

Creating a complete community here means balancing complex technical, social, and regulatory hurdles within a limited footprint.

So, who lives here now?

Cooksville is Mississauga's quintessential gateway. A vibrant, first-stop neighbourhood for nearly 20,000 people, where 1 in 5 residents is a recent arrival.

Two-thirds of its residents are foreign-born (66.5%), and its median age is a youthful 37.4.

It's also a community of renters: over half of all residents rent (50.7%), with most living in high-rise apartments (68.1%).

Two-thirds of households are families, alongside a growing number of seniors of whom many live alone.

Public life is centered at the 'Four Corners' of Hurontario and Dundas.

With few formal civic amenities, residents rely on commercial plazas and small parks for daily needs.

Beyond this intersection, amenities quickly thin out, creating a gap between density and daily life.

It is also a socio-economically poorer area.

Household incomes average about $92,000 per year, starkly lower than the city’s average.
